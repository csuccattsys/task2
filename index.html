The Rule of Proximity

When distinct sentence subjects are chained using either...or or neither...nor, stop trying to count all the elements together.

Instead, focus entirely on the subject closest to the verb position.

  • If that nearest subject is singular → use a singular verb.
  • If that nearest subject is plural → use a plural verb.

Case 1: Closest Subject is Singular

Let's look at what happens when the second subject is singular:

"Neither the teachers nor the principal is attending the meeting today."

Why? Even though "teachers" is plural, the word principal sits right next to the verb action. Since principal is singular, we drop in the singular verb is.

Case 2: Closest Subject is Plural

Now let's swap the word order around and notice how the verb structurally responds:

"Either the principal or the teachers are attending the meeting today."

Why? Now, the plural noun teachers sits closest to the action window. Therefore, the verb naturally transforms into the matching plural variant, are.
